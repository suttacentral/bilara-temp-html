Theravāda Vinaya Theravāda Collection on Monastic Law

Mahāvibhaṅga The monks’ rules and their analysis

Nissaggiyakaṇḍa The chapter on relinquishment

Cīvaravagga The sub-chapter on robes

7. Tatuttarisikkhāpada 7. The training rule on more than that

Origin story

Tena samayena buddho bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. At one time the Buddha was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. Tena kho pana samayena chabbaggiyā bhikkhū acchinnacīvarake bhikkhū upasaṅkamitvā evaṃ vadanti— At that time the monks from the group of six said this to the monks whose robes had been stolen, “bhagavatā, āvuso, anuññātaṃ— “The Master ‘acchinnacīvarassa vā naṭṭhacīvarassa vā aññātakaṃ gahapatiṃ vā gahapatāniṃ vā cīvaraṃ viññāpetuṃ’; has allowed a monk whose robes have been stolen or destroyed to ask an unrelated lay person for robe-cloth. viññāpetha, āvuso, cīvaran”ti. You should ask for robe-cloth.”

“Alaṃ, āvuso, laddhaṃ amhehi cīvaran”ti. “It’s not necessary; we have already obtained robes.”

“Mayaṃ āyasmantānaṃ viññāpemā”ti. “We’ll ask for you.”

“Viññāpetha, āvuso”ti. “Do as you please.”

Atha kho chabbaggiyā bhikkhū gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā etadavocuṃ— Then the monks from the group of six went to lay people and said, “acchinnacīvarakā, āvuso, bhikkhū āgatā. “Monks have arrived whose robes have been stolen. Detha nesaṃ cīvarānī”ti, Give robes for them.” bahuṃ cīvaraṃ viññāpesuṃ. And they asked for much robe-cloth.

Tena kho pana samayena aññataro puriso sabhāyaṃ nisinno aññataraṃ purisaṃ etadavoca— Soon afterwards in the public meeting hall a man said to another, “acchinnacīvarakā ayyo bhikkhū āgatā. “Sir, monks have arrived whose robes have been stolen. Tesaṃ mayā cīvaraṃ dinnan”ti. I’ve given them robe-cloth.”

Sopi evamāha—“mayāpi dinnan”ti. “I, too, have given robe-cloth.” Aparopi evamāha—“mayāpi dinnan”ti. And another said the same.

Te ujjhāyanti khiyyanti vipācenti— They grumbled and complained, “kathañhi nāma samaṇā sakyaputtiyā na mattaṃ jānitvā bahuṃ cīvaraṃ viññāpessanti, “How can the Sakyan ascetics have no sense of moderation and ask for much robe-cloth? dussavāṇijjaṃ vā samaṇā sakyaputtiyā karissanti, paggāhikasālaṃ vā pasāressantī”ti. Will they start up as cloth merchants or set up shop?”

Assosuṃ kho bhikkhū tesaṃ manussānaṃ ujjhāyantānaṃ khiyyantānaṃ vipācentānaṃ. The monks heard the complaints of those people, Ye te bhikkhū appicchā … pe … te ujjhāyanti khiyyanti vipācenti— and the monks of few desires complained and criticized those monks, “kathañhi nāma chabbaggiyā bhikkhū na mattaṃ jānitvā bahuṃ cīvaraṃ viññāpessantī”ti. “How can the monks from the group of six not have any sense of moderation and ask for much robe-cloths?”

Atha kho te bhikkhū chabbaggiye bhikkhū anekapariyāyena vigarahitvā bhagavato etamatthaṃ ārocesuṃ … pe … After criticizing those monks in many ways, they told the Master … “saccaṃ kira tumhe, bhikkhave, na mattaṃ jānitvā bahuṃ cīvaraṃ viññāpethā”ti? “Is it true, monks, that you acted like this?”

“Saccaṃ, bhagavā”ti. “It’s true, Master.”

Vigarahi buddho bhagavā … pe … The Buddha rebuked them, “… kathañhi nāma tumhe, moghapurisā, na mattaṃ jānitvā bahuṃ cīvaraṃ viññāpessatha. Foolish men, how can you not have any sense of moderation and ask for much robe-cloth? Netaṃ, moghapurisā, appasannānaṃ vā pasādāya … pe … This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … evañca pana, bhikkhave, imaṃ sikkhāpadaṃ uddiseyyātha— And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

“Tañce aññātako gahapati vā gahapatānī vā bahūhi cīvarehi abhihaṭṭhuṃ pavāreyya santaruttaraparamaṃ tena bhikkhunā tato cīvaraṃ sāditabbaṃ. Tato ce uttari sādiyeyya, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyan”ti. ‘If an unrelated layman or laywoman invites that monk to take much robe-cloth, he should accept at most cloth for one sarong and one upper robe. If he accepts more than that, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession.’”

Definitions

Tañceti That monk:
acchinnacīvarakaṃ bhikkhuṃ. the monk whose robes have been stolen.
Aññātako nāma Unrelated:
mātito vā pitito vā yāva sattamā pitāmahayugā asambaddho. anyone who is not a descendant of one’s male ancestors going back seven generations, either on the mother’s side or on the father’s side.
Gahapati nāma A layman:
yo koci agāraṃ ajjhāvasati. any man who lives in a house.
Gahapatānī nāma A laywoman:
yā kāci agāraṃ ajjhāvasati. any woman who lives in a house.
Bahūhi cīvarehīti Much robe-cloth:
bahukehi cīvarehi. a lot of robe-cloth.
Abhihaṭṭhuṃ pavāreyyāti Invites […] to take:
yāvatakaṃ icchasi tāvatakaṃ gaṇhāhīti. saying, “Take as many as you like.”
Santaruttaraparamaṃ tena bhikkhunā tato cīvaraṃ sāditabbanti He should accept at most robe-cloth for one sarong and one upper robe:
sace tīṇi naṭṭhāni honti dve sāditabbāni, dve naṭṭhāni ekaṃ sāditabbaṃ, ekaṃ naṭṭhaṃ na kiñci sāditabbaṃ. if three robes are destroyed, he should accept robe-cloth for two; if two robes are destroyed, he should accept robe-cloth for one; if one robe is destroyed, he should not accept any robe-cloth.
Tato ce uttari sādiyeyyāti If he accepts more than that:
tatuttari viññāpeti, payoge dukkaṭaṃ. if he asks for more than that, then the effort constitutes wrong conduct. Paṭilābhena nissaggiyaṃ hoti. When he gets the robe-cloth, it becomes subject to relinquishment.

Nissajjitabbaṃ saṃghassa vā gaṇassa vā puggalassa vā. The robe-cloth should be relinquished to an Order, a group, or an individual. Evañca pana, bhikkhave, nissajjitabbaṃ. “And, monks, it should be relinquished in this way. … pe … To be expanded as in [Relinquishment 1, paragraphs 13–17](pli-tv-bu-vb-np1#13), with appropriate substitutions.

“idaṃ me, bhante, cīvaraṃ aññātakaṃ gahapatikaṃ upasaṅkamitvā tatuttari viññāpitaṃ nissaggiyaṃ. ‘Venerables, this robe-cloth which I received after asking an unrelated lay person for too much is to be relinquished. Imāhaṃ saṃghassa nissajjāmī”ti … pe … I relinquish it to the Order.’ … dadeyyāti … pe … the Order should give … dadeyyunti … pe … you should give … āyasmato dammīti. ‘I give this robe-cloth back to you.’”

Permutations

Aññātake aññātakasaññī tatuttari cīvaraṃ viññāpeti, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the person is unrelated and the monk perceives them as unrelated, and he asks them for too much robe-cloth, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession. Aññātake vematiko tatuttari cīvaraṃ viññāpeti, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the person is unrelated, but the monk is unsure if they are, and he asks them for too much robe-cloth, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession. Aññātake ñātakasaññī tatuttari cīvaraṃ viññāpeti, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the person is unrelated, but the monk perceives them as related, and he asks them for too much robe-cloth, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession.

Ñātake aññātakasaññī, āpatti dukkaṭassa. If the person is related, but the monk perceives them as unrelated, commits an offense of wrong conduct. Ñātake vematiko, āpatti dukkaṭassa. If the person is related, but the monk is unsure if they are, commits an offense of wrong conduct. Ñātake ñātakasaññī, anāpatti. If the person is related and the monk perceives them as related, there is no offense.

Non-offenses

Anāpatti— There is no offense: “sesakaṃ āharissāmī”ti haranto gacchati, if he takes too much, but with the intention of returning the remainder; “sesakaṃ tuyheva hotū”ti denti, if they give, saying, “The remainder is for you;” na acchinnakāraṇā denti, if they give, but not because his robes were stolen; na naṭṭhakāraṇā denti, if they give, but not because his robes were destroyed; ñātakānaṃ, if it is from relatives; pavāritānaṃ, if it is from those who have given an invitation; attano dhanena, if it is by means of his own property; ummattakassa, if he is insane; ādikammikassāti. if he is the first offender.

Tatuttarisikkhāpadaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ sattamaṃ. The seventh rule, the training rule on more than that, is finished.
