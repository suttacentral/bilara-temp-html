Theravāda Vinaya Theravāda Collection on Monastic Law

Mahāvibhaṅga The monks’ rules and their analysis

Nissaggiyakaṇḍa The chapter on relinquishment

Cīvaravagga The sub-chapter on robes

9. Dutiyaupakkhaṭasikkhāpada 9. The second training rule on what is set aside

Origin story

Tena samayena buddho bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. At one time the Buddha was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. Tena kho pana samayena aññataro puriso aññataraṃ purisaṃ etadavoca— At that time a certain man said to another man, “ayyaṃ upanandaṃ cīvarena acchādessāmī”ti. “I“ll give a robe to Venerable Upananda.” Sopi evamāha— And he replied, “ahampi ayyaṃ upanandaṃ cīvarena acchādessāmī”ti. “I, too, will give a robe to Venerable Upananda.”

Assosi kho aññataro piṇḍacāriko bhikkhu tesaṃ purisānaṃ imaṃ kathāsallāpaṃ. A monk who was an alms-collector heard that conversation. Atha kho so bhikkhu yenāyasmā upanando sakyaputto tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmantaṃ upanandaṃ sakyaputtaṃ etadavoca— He then went to Upananda and said, “mahāpuññosi tvaṃ, āvuso upananda. “Upananda, you have much merit. Amukasmiṃ okāse aññataro puriso aññataraṃ purisaṃ etadavoca— I’ve just heard two men telling each other ‘ayyaṃ upanandaṃ cīvarena acchādessāmī’ti. that they will each give you a robe.” Sopi evamāha— ‘ahampi ayyaṃ upanandaṃ cīvarena acchādessāmī’”ti.

“Atthāvuso, maṃ te upaṭṭhākā”ti. “They are my supporters.”

Atha kho āyasmā upanando sakyaputto yena te purisā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā te purise etadavoca— Upananda then went to those men and said, “saccaṃ kira maṃ tumhe, āvuso, cīvarehi acchādetukāmātthā”ti? “Is it true that you both desire to give me a robe?”

“Api nayya, evaṃ hoti— “Indeed, ‘ayyaṃ upanandaṃ cīvarehi acchādessāmā’”ti. that’s just what we were thinking.”

“Sace kho maṃ tumhe, āvuso, cīvarehi acchādetukāmāttha, evarūpena cīvarena acchādetha, “If that’s the case, then give me such-and-such a robe. kyāhaṃ tehi acchannopi karissāmi, yānāhaṃ na paribhuñjissāmī”ti. For what’s the point of giving robes that I won’t use?”

Atha kho te purisā ujjhāyanti khiyyanti vipācenti— Those men grumbled and complained, “mahicchā ime samaṇā sakyaputtiyā asantuṭṭhā. “These Sakyan ascetics have great desires; they are not content. Nayime sukarā cīvarehi acchādetuṃ. It’s no easy matter to give them robes. Kathañhi nāma ayyo upanando amhehi pubbe appavārito upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjissatī”ti. How can Venerable Upananda approach us and specify the kind of robe he wants without first being invited?”

Assosuṃ kho bhikkhū tesaṃ purisānaṃ ujjhāyantānaṃ khiyyantānaṃ vipācentānaṃ. The monks heard the complaints of those men, Ye te bhikkhū appicchā … pe … te ujjhāyanti khiyyanti vipācenti— and the monks of few desires complained and criticized him, “kathañhi nāma āyasmā upanando sakyaputto pubbe appavārito gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjissatī”ti. “How can Venerable Upananda approach lay people and make a suggestion about a robe without first being invited?”

Atha kho te bhikkhū āyasmantaṃ upanandaṃ sakyaputtaṃ anekapariyāyena vigarahitvā bhagavato etamatthaṃ ārocesuṃ … pe … After criticizing him in many ways, they told the Master … “saccaṃ kira tvaṃ, upananda, pubbe appavārito gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjasī”ti? “Is it true, Upananda, that you acted like this?”

“Saccaṃ, bhagavā”ti. “It’s true, Master.”

“Ñātakā te, upananda, aññātakā”ti? “Are they relatives of yours?”

“Aññātakā, bhagavā”ti. “No, Master.”

“Aññātako, moghapurisa, aññātakānaṃ na jānāti patirūpaṃ vā appatirūpaṃ vā santaṃ vā asantaṃ vā. “Foolish man, people who are not related don’t know what’s appropriate and what’s inappropriate in dealing with each other, what’s right and what’s wrong. Tattha nāma tvaṃ, moghapurisa, pubbe appavārito aññātake gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjissasi. And still you approached unrelated lay people and specified the kind of robe you wanted without first being invited. Netaṃ, moghapurisa, appasannānaṃ vā pasādāya … pe … This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … evañca pana, bhikkhave, imaṃ sikkhāpadaṃ uddiseyyātha— And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

“Bhikkhuṃ paneva uddissa ubhinnaṃ aññātakānaṃ gahapatīnaṃ vā gahapatānīnaṃ vā paccekacīvaracetāpannāni upakkhaṭāni honti— ‘If two male or female lay people have set aside separate robe funds for an unrelated monk, thinking, ‘imehi mayaṃ paccekacīvaracetāpannehi paccekacīvarāni cetāpetvā itthannāmaṃ bhikkhuṃ cīvarehi acchādessāmā’ti; ‘With these separate robe funds we will buy separate robes and give them to monk so-and-so;’ tatra ce so bhikkhu pubbe appavārito upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjeyya— and if that monk, without first being invited, approaches them and specifies the kind of robe he wants, saying, ‘sādhu vata maṃ āyasmanto imehi paccekacīvaracetāpannehi evarūpaṃ vā evarūpaṃ vā cīvaraṃ cetāpetvā acchādetha, ubhova santā ekenā’ti, ‘It would be good if you would put these separate robe funds together to buy such-and-such a robe and then give it to me,’ kalyāṇakamyataṃ upādāya, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyan”ti. and he does so because he wants something nice, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession.’”

Definitions

Bhikkhuṃ paneva uddissāti For a […] monk:
bhikkhussatthāya, bhikkhuṃ ārammaṇaṃ karitvā, bhikkhuṃ acchādetukāmā. for the benefit of a monk; making a monk the object of consideration, they desire to give to him.
Ubhinnanti Two:
dvinnaṃ. a pair.
Aññātakā nāma Unrelated:
mātito vā pitito vā yāva sattamā pitāmahayugā asambaddhā. anyone who is not a descendant of one’s male ancestors going back seven generations, either on the mother’s side or on the father’s side.
Gahapatī nāma Male lay people:
ye keci agāraṃ ajjhāvasanti. any men who live in a house.
Gahapatāniyo nāma Female lay people:
yā kāci agāraṃ ajjhāvasanti. any women who live in a house.
Cīvaracetāpannāni nāma Robe funds:
hiraññā vā suvaṇṇā vā muttā vā maṇī vā pavāḷā vā phalikā vā paṭakā vā suttā vā kappāsā vā. money, gold, pearls, gems, corals, crystals, cloth, thread, or cotton wool.
Imehi paccekacīvaracetāpannehīti With these separate robe funds:
paccupaṭṭhitehi. with that which is at their disposal.
Cetāpetvāti We will buy:
parivattetvā. having exchanged.
Acchādessāmāti We will […] give:
dassāma. we will donate.
Tatra ce so bhikkhūti If that monk:
yaṃ bhikkhuṃ uddissa cīvaracetāpannāni upakkhaṭāni honti so bhikkhu. the monk for whom the robe funds have been set aside.
Pubbe appavāritoti Without first being invited:
pubbe avutto hoti— “kīdisena te, bhante, cīvarena attho, kīdisaṃ te cīvaraṃ cetāpemā”ti. they have not said beforehand: “Venerable, what kind of robe do you need? What kind of robe can we buy for you?”
Upasaṅkamitvāti Approaches them:
gharaṃ gantvā yattha katthaci upasaṅkamitvā. having gone to their house or having gone wherever.
Cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjeyyāti Specifies the kind of robe he wants:
āyataṃ vā hotu vitthataṃ vā appitaṃ vā saṇhaṃ vā. please make it long or wide or closely woven or soft.
Imehi paccekacīvaracetāpannehīti These separate robe funds:
paccupaṭṭhitehi. that which is at their disposal.
Evarūpaṃ vā evarūpaṃ vāti Such-and-such:
āyataṃ vā vitthataṃ vā appitaṃ vā saṇhaṃ vā. long or wide or closely woven or soft.
Cetāpetvāti To buy:
parivattetvā. having exchanged.
Acchādethāti Give:
dajjetha. donate.
Ubhova santā ekenāti Put […] together:
dvepi janā ekena. two people supplying him with one robe.
Kalyāṇakamyataṃ upādāyāti Because he wants something nice:

sādhatthiko mahagghatthiko. wanting something nice, wanting something expensive.

Tassa vacanena āyataṃ vā vitthataṃ vā appitaṃ vā saṇhaṃ vā cetāpenti, payoge dukkaṭaṃ. If those lay people buy a robe that is long, wide, closely woven, or soft because of his statement, then the effort constitutes wrong conduct. Paṭilābhena nissaggiyaṃ hoti. When he gets the robe, it becomes subject to relinquishment.

Nissajjitabbaṃ saṃghassa vā gaṇassa vā puggalassa vā. The robe should be relinquished to an Order, a group, or an individual. Evañca pana, bhikkhave, nissajjitabbaṃ. “And, monks, it should be relinquished in this way. … pe … To be expanded as in [Relinquishment 1, paragraphs 13–17](pli-tv-bu-vb-np1#13), with appropriate substitutions.

“idaṃ me, bhante, cīvaraṃ pubbe appavārito aññātake gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpannaṃ nissaggiyaṃ. ‘Venerables, this robe which I received after approaching unrelated lay people and specifying the kind of robe I wanted without first being invited is to be relinquished. Imāhaṃ saṃghassa nissajjāmī”ti … pe … I relinquish it to the Order.’ … dadeyyāti … pe … the Order should give … dadeyyunti … pe … you should give … āyasmato dammīti. ‘I give this robe back to you.’”

Permutations

Aññātake aññātakasaññī pubbe appavārito gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjati, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the lay people are unrelated and he perceives them as unrelated, and, without first being invited, he approaches them and specifies the kind of robe he wants, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession. Aññātake vematiko pubbe appavārito gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjati, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the lay people are unrelated, but he is unsure if they are, and, without first being invited, he approaches them and specifies the kind of robe he wants, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession. Aññātake ñātakasaññī pubbe appavārito gahapatike upasaṅkamitvā cīvare vikappaṃ āpajjati, nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ. If the lay people are unrelated, but he perceives them as related, and, without first being invited, he approaches them and specifies the kind of robe he wants, he commits an offense entailing relinquishment and confession.

Ñātake aññātakasaññī, āpatti dukkaṭassa. If the lay people are related, but he perceives them as unrelated, commits an offense of wrong conduct. Ñātake vematiko, āpatti dukkaṭassa. If the lay people are related, but he is unsure if they are, commits an offense of wrong conduct. Ñātake ñātakasaññī, anāpatti. If the lay people are related and he perceives them as related, there is no offense.

Non-offenses

Anāpatti— There is no offense: ñātakānaṃ, if it is from relatives; pavāritānaṃ, if it is from those who have given an invitation; aññassatthāya, if it is for the benefit of another; attano dhanena, if it is by means of his own property; mahagghaṃ cetāpetukāmānaṃ appagghaṃ cetāpeti, if the lay people wish to buy something expensive, but he gets them to buy something inexpensive; ummattakassa, if he is insane; ādikammikassāti. if he is the first offender.

Dutiyaupakkhaṭasikkhāpadaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ navamaṃ. The ninth rule, the second training rule on what is set aside, is finished.
