Theravāda Vinaya Theravāda Collection on Monastic Law

Mahāvibhaṅga The monks’ rules and their analysis

Sekhiyakaṇḍa The chapter on training

Parimaṇḍalavagga The sub-chapter on evenly all around

5. Susaṃvutasikkhāpada 5. The training rule on well-restrained

Origin story

Tena samayena buddho bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. At one time the Buddha was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. Tena kho pana samayena chabbaggiyā bhikkhū hatthampi pādampi kīḷāpentā antaraghare gacchanti … At that time the monks from the group of six were walking in inhabited areas, playing with their hands and feet. …

Final ruling

“Susaṃvuto antaraghare gamissāmīti sikkhā karaṇīyā”ti. “‘I will walk well-restrained in inhabited areas,’ this is how you should train.”

Susaṃvutena antaraghare gantabbaṃ. Walking in an inhabited area, one should be well-restrained. Yo anādariyaṃ paṭicca hatthaṃ vā pādaṃ vā kīḷāpento antaraghare gacchati, āpatti dukkaṭassa. If a monk, out of disrespect, walks in an inhabited area, playing with his hands and feet, he commits an offense of wrong conduct.

Non-offenses

Anāpatti— There is no offense: asañcicca, if it is unintentional; assatiyā, if he is not mindful; ajānantassa, if he does not know; gilānassa, if he is sick; ummattakassa, if he is insane; ādikammikassāti. if he is the first offender.

Pañcamasikkhāpadaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ. The fifth training rule is finished.
